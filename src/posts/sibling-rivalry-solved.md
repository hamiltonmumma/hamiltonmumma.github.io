"If Ethan gets one more point than me, I'm done!"

That was my 12-year-old niece, mid-tantrum, three weeks into testing ScholarQuest with our family. She's competitive — maybe too competitive. Her older sister (15) had been racking up points through reading challenges, and my niece was frustrated she couldn't catch up.

Sound familiar?

Here's what happened next: I showed her the family leaderboard settings.

## The Problem With Sibling Competition

Every parent knows the drill. Two kids, one cookie. Two kids, one controller. Two kids, one opportunity to one-up each other. Sibling rivalry is *normal*, but when you're trying to get your kids to learn, it can become a nightmare:

- The older kid always wins → the younger gives up
- Comparisons happen constantly → "Why can't you be like your sister?"
- Rewards become zero-sum → if one wins, the other loses

Traditional gamification makes this worse. Leaderboards with a single winner? That's a recipe for tears.

## What We Built Instead

ScholarQuest's family leaderboards work differently. Here's how to set them up so competition actually *motivates* instead of discouraging:

### 1. Split the Leaderboard by Age Group

In your family dashboard, you can create separate leaderboard views. My nephew (9) competes against other 9-year-olds. My older niece (15) is in her own bracket. Same family, different leaderboards.

The 9-year-old now has a realistic shot at the top spot. He's climbed from last place to #2 in his group in two weeks — entirely because he *can* win.

**How to set it up:** Go to Family → Settings → Leaderboard Groups → "Split by Age"

### 2. Turn Off Points, Turn On Streaks

Points create a zero-sum game. Streaks create a growth mindset.

Instead of "who has the most points," switch to "who's maintained their streak longest." When my nieces stopped comparing raw scores and started cheering each other on for keeping a 14-day reading streak? Game changer.

Now they ask each other: "Did you do your challenge today?" — not "How many points do you have?"

**How to set it up:** Family → Dashboard → View → Toggle "Streak Mode"

### 3. Team Challenges Are Your Secret Weapon

The best thing we discovered during testing: **team challenges**. Put siblings on the same team against... nobody. Just themselves.

Here's a real example: Last weekend, the three kids (ages 9, 12, 15) teamed up for a "Family Reading Marathon" — each had to read 30 minutes, and they had to hit 90 total minutes together. They did. The 15-year-old helped the 9-year-old with chapter summaries. The 12-year-old kept track of the timer. No competition at all. Just teamwork.

**How to set it up:** Challenges → Create Family Challenge → Toggle "Team Mode"

## The Real Result

Three weeks later, my niece (the one who threatened to quit) asked if she could create a custom challenge for her sister. She's now the one *proposing* learning activities — because she found a way to compete that doesn't feel hopeless.

That's the goal here. Not "one kid wins, one kid loses." It's "everyone is moving forward, and we're all watching each other grow."
